Is Free Trade Bad for America?

(image taken from Bloomberg Businessweek Europe, available here.)

 

I usually start my posts with a motivating picture.

This cartoon from Bloomberg encapsulates how Trump wants to do trade with other countries:  He basically wants to kick them out of America.  Trump says that American workers are losing their jobs because they are being outsourced to other countries like Mexico and China.

Let's think of a basic case: steel.  Production of steel is cheaper in China, rather than the US.  Because of free trade and globalization, Chinese steel can make its way to the US and still come out cheaper than steel produced in America.  As a result, consumers that prefer to buy cheaper will choose to buy Chinese steel, at the expense of American steelworkers.

So if you've been reading Trump repealing TPP (Trans-Pacific Partnership) and NAFTA (North-American Free Trade Agreement), that's essentially his point.  There are a lot of products that one can think of wherein the common American worker is losing its edge: car production, garments,  electronics, etc.  If Mr. Trump promised to bring back jobs to America, then he must "kill the competition."

To be fair, there have been times when these free trade agreements with America have been abused.  For instance, some countries build manufacturing plants in Mexico, and in turn uses Mexico's NAFTA agreement with the US in order to circumvent quotas.  And if there is one thing that Mr. Trump hates, it is being outsmarted.

But in any case, Trump's view on trade is this: These free trade agreements are causing inequality in America.  The richest Americans (the 1%) are pulling away at an exponential rate while the common worker (whose jobs are lost or threatened due to outsourcing) is left behind.

I was asked to investigate whether this claim is justified.
