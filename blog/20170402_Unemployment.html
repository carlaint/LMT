Is USA's unemployment really that HUUUUUGE?

April 2, 2017

One of my new pastimes -- more like a pastime at the office -- is to validate the outlandish claims of the US president on almost every topic he can get his hands on.

The topic for today is US unemployment. He had recently made a statement.    

Now, a lot of media outlets have fact-checked this statement, and most if not all, debunked the claim. For students of economics, unemployment is not the same as "out of the labor force." 96 million includes students, retirees, disabled people, etc. who are not looking for a job. You can't possibly include your grandma aged 62 or your child aged 16 as part of the unemployed!

Regardless, I went to work and ran the numbers. I compared the US numbers with other advanced economies, to see whether US unemployment is really a staggering number compared to others.

  It's a very simple research question, but I think it's good to tackle it systematically, because admittedly, it is very easy to get lost in the terminology. First, it is important to distinguish between the Working-Age Population (WAP) and the Non-WAP. Simply, the working age is globally defined by the International Labor Organization as the population from 15-65 years old. From the working age population, we can then define who is "in the labor force" and who is not.

For this experiment I had to use 2014 data because of complete data (Working age, Non-Working age, labor force information) availability. The viz tells you that the American workforce dwarfs the others. 171 million, or 53.66% of the American population is in the labor force. From this number, we can check how many people are employed and unemployed.

OECD numbers show that 68.8% of the working age population is employed, which is approximately 145 million Americans. I would be very careful with reading the definitions, because any misstep would be misleading. The OECD definition is based on the working age population and not on the labor force. Regardless, this implies that the actual unemployment figure, based on this computation is only 26 million. This is way off from Mr. Trump's claim of 94 million unemployed Americans. (Never mind the 2014/2016 time mismatch: 94 million is still way off from 26 million). And even if you add the number of those "not in the labor force", the number still comes to (26 million + 40 million) 64 million. I would find it hard to believe that in two years time, 30 million jobs were lost, especially at a time when it is reported that unemployment in America is back to pre-crisis levels.
