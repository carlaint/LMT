Is USA's unemployment really that HUUUUUGE?

April 2, 2017

One of my new pastimes is to use the IMF's WEO data to validate the outlandish claims of the US president on almost every topic he can get his hands on.

The topic for today is US labor statistics. He had recently made a statement:

This is a staggering soundbyte, and quite a compelling number:

94 million! Out of the labor force!

But the rhetoric is rather strategic, arguably masterfully crafted to deceive.

It is quite convenient to misconstrue "Out of the labor force" to mean "Unemployment", but they mean two different things.

I thought that the exercise may be a good first foray into the infographics realm. I created the one below to explain the differences in terminology.

The hairy definitions

The total population can be divided into two broad categories:

  1. Non-Working Age Population, or those who are too young or too old to work; and
  2. Working-Age Population,which is the legally-allowed age of employment. In most countries, the Working Age population is from 15-65 years old.

Then, one can further break down Working Age Population into:

  1. "Not in the Labor Force," like students who are in school / university and choose not to work; and
  2. "In the Labor Force," which includes: those who are working (employed), or those looking for work (unemployed).

Clearly, it is misleading to assume that "Not in the Labor Force" is the same as "Unemployed." There is not even a definitional overlap between these two terms, and each refers to something very different. The visual above only shows 40 million Americans out of the labor force. (Granted that these are 2014 numbers, there is still a big difference between 40 and 94 million.)

So where did the 94 million come from?

I would argue that numbers were added from the Non-Working Age Population. Technically speaking, those who are below the age of 15 or above the age of 65 are also not in the labor force. I mean, technically, a typical kindergartner would not be in the labor force, right? So the only feasible assumption is that, these numbers were added to inflate the effect.

What about the unemployed?

If you look at the visual, American unemployment is not a huuuuge chunk of the population. The red bar (unemployed) in comparison to the green bar (employed) is a small percentage, especially when compared to countries like Spain and Italy which has chronic unemployment problems. This tells us that the share of unemployed persons in the US, in relation to those employed, especially when compared to its OECD peers, is not as bad as Mr. Trump likes to make it sound.

Reference

IMF's World Economic Outlook database. Available on data.imf.org; OECDStat; and the US Bureau of Labor Statistics.
