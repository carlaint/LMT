Are they catching up to my income level?

April 5, 2017

The point of this exercise is to benchmark a person's income with another. Ideally, this can be done by selecting two countries and comparing their respective population segments (do you belong to the top 10%? the middle 10%? or the bottom 10%?). This data is taken from the World Bank's Povcal database, disclaimers apply (see bottom of the page).

Quick start tip
Select your country and the decile of your monthly income level, using the slider to toggle between deciles. (For example, if I live in the United States and I earned roughly $900 per month in 2012, then I would classify myself in decile 3.) Then choose a country of interest in Country 2 to compare.

Explore on your own!

  • The default view above compares the incomes of China's richest 10% with an American whose income level is in the lowest 30% of the population. We can see that the wealthy Chinese start off in the 1980s, significantly earning lower than the poorest 30% of Americans. The wealthiest 10% in China have steadily grown their income, to the point of overtaking the income level of the US' poorest 30% in 2010. The poorest 30% in the United States is seen to have a stagnant pace of growth, while the Chinese have continued to grow.

  • Comparing the middle 10% of my home country, the Philippines, with the middle 10% of our neighbor, Thailand, I could observe that the average Filipino has been lagging behind the Thais since the 1980s. Both countries are on an upward trajectory, which implies that incomes for the middle-class are rising, but Thailand income levels are still higher than Philippine incomes. (Which can partly explain why there are a lot of overseas Filipino workers in Thailand!)

Reference

World Bank PovcalNet: An online analysis tool for global poverty monitoring. Accessed May 2017.

Disclaimer: The Povcal database is used by the World Bank to provide poverty estimates. The authors warn against alternative uses of the data.
