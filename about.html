About LMT

The idea for the title "Lame Man's Terms (LMT)" began as a conversation with my better half about creating a YouTube channel.  I thought that he explained his science--he is a neuroscientist-- in a fascinating and plain-English way that someone like me (not exactly the biggest fan of science) could understand.  He wasn't too keen on it.  The conversation went like this:

Me:  "I want to create a YouTube channel where you could be the host and you can explain science in LAYMAN's terms"

Adam (sarcastically): "Yes, yes.. exactly.. in LAME man terms!"

Obviously, the YouTube channel didn't work out, but I thought it would be a good title for this lingering idea in my head: to build a platform where I could share the interesting work and data visualizations that I do as a macroeconomics researcher at the IMF. Hence, this blog.

Admittedly, macroeconomics is a topic that puts people to sleep.  It is not exactly the sexiest of all fields. A lot of people are unwilling to understand it, because it is easy to get lost in the jargon.  There is a saying in our science that "You don't really care about macroeconomics until you've lost your job."  It's true.  It's hard to grasp the concepts of inflation and exchange rates, and apply it to everyday life.

My goal with this blog is to bridge this gap:

To explain macro topics using interactive data visualizations that people can experiment with and understand.

To reduce the jargon and demonstrate macroeconomics concepts in the most user-friendly way

To make the topic accessible to the layman

To use the applied sciences (data science and statistics) in simplifying macro concepts, without losing the main idea

Generally, to make people care about macroeconomics and why it matters.

Who am I?

I am a researcher at the International Monetary Fund. I have juggled a number of roles at the IMF, from a Research Analyst formulating strategies for developing economies, to Information Officer validating data submissions by member countries.  Currently, my official title is Research Assistant, and I support the Deputy Managing Director -- a high-ranking official at the IMF.

The cool thing about my job is that I get to collaborate on messaging and ideation with a member of top management.  This enables me to innovate and experiment with data analytics and visualizations.  My unique position as junior staff also makes me a flexible member of the team.  At times I wear many hats.   There are days that I am an economist, or a data guru, a statistician, a storyteller and speechwriter, or a coffee preparer.  I sometimes stir all these roles together and think of myself as a data scientist.

My background

I am an economist by training.  I did my undergraduate studies in Economics at the University of the Philippines in 2004.  After a few years of working in the private sector back home, I went to graduate school and completed an M.A. in Applied Economics at the Johns Hopkins University in 2011.
